```markdown
## II. Theoretical Foundations

This section develops five theoretical strands that converge to explain the funding-growth paradox.

### Strand A: Commitment and Strategy

**Classical Origin.** Ghemawat (1991) established commitment as central to strategy: irreversible investments create competitive advantage by signaling resolve. Porter (1996) reinforced this, arguing strategic positioning requires trade-offs.

**Development.** Real options theory challenged this orthodoxy. Trigeorgis (1996) showed that under uncertainty, flexibility has option value. Dixit and Pindyck (1994) demonstrated the value of waiting to invest.

**Current Follow-on.** McGrath (1999) extended real options to entrepreneurship, reframing failures as "falling forward." Adner and Levinthal (2004) clarified boundaries: not everything is a real option.

### Strand B: Learning and Adaptation

**Classical Origin.** Cyert and March (1963) established organizations as adaptive systems. March (1991) posed the exploitation-exploration tension. Nelson and Winter (1982) formalized evolutionary dynamics.

**Development.** Levinthal and March (1993) identified the "myopia of learning"—organizations learn locally, missing distant opportunities. Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) introduced dynamic capabilities.

**Current Follow-on.** Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) operationalized dynamic capabilities. Camuffo et al. (2020) showed scientific approaches improve venture learning.

### Strand C: Governance and Agency

**Classical Origin.** Jensen and Meckling (1976) formalized agency theory: separation of ownership and control creates conflicts.

**Development.** Sahlman (1990) applied this to venture capital: staging and governance mitigate agency costs. Gompers and Lerner (2001) documented VC's role in professionalization.

**Current Follow-on.** Hellmann and Puri (2002) showed VC involvement accelerates professionalization but also constrains founder autonomy.

### Strand D: Identity and Strategic Change

**Classical Origin.** Penrose (1959) established firm growth depends on managerial resources and vision. Barney (1991) formalized the resource-based view.

**Development.** Sanchez (1995) linked flexibility to competitive dynamics. O'Reilly and Tushman (2008) introduced ambidexterity—simultaneous exploitation and exploration.

**Current Follow-on.** Zuzul and Tripsas (2020) explain adaptation variation through founder identity. Grimes (2018) documented how founders respond to feedback through identity work.

### Strand E: Growth and Scaling

**Classical Origin.** Fine (1998) introduced "clockspeed"—the rate at which industries evolve. Firms must match their operational tempo to industry dynamics.

**Development.** Gawer and Cusumano (2014) extended this to platforms: scaling requires coordinating ecosystem growth with internal capability development.

**Current Follow-on.** Recent practitioner work (Ries, 2011; Blank, 2013) operationalized scaling for startups: growth requires parallel expansion of market reach and delivery capability.
```

### Literature Review Section (Strand Format)

```markdown
## II. Theoretical Foundations

This section develops five theoretical strands that converge to explain the funding-growth paradox.

### Strand A: Commitment and Strategy

**Classical Origin.** Ghemawat (1991) established commitment as central to strategy: irreversible investments create competitive advantage by signaling resolve and deterring rivals. Porter (1996) reinforced this view, arguing that strategic positioning requires trade-offs that competitors cannot easily imitate.

**Development.** The real options literature challenged this orthodoxy. Dixit and Pindyck (1994) demonstrated that under uncertainty, flexibility has option value—the right to wait and learn before committing. McGrath (1999) extended this logic to entrepreneurship, reframing failures as "falling forward": valuable options that expired worthless but generated learning.

**Current Follow-on.** Recent work synthesizes these tensions. Stern (2024) models entrepreneurship as Bayesian updating where founders hold contrarian beliefs and learn through experimentation. The challenge, as Gans, Stern, and Wu (2019) formalize, is that commitment-free analysis often yields multiple equally viable strategies—forcing entrepreneurs to *choose* rather than *optimize*.

### Strand B: Learning and Adaptation

**Classical Origin.** March (1991) posed the fundamental tension: organizations must balance exploitation of current capabilities against exploration of new possibilities. Too much exploitation leads to competency traps; too much exploration wastes resources on unproven alternatives.

**Development.** Organizational learning research revealed systematic barriers to adaptation. Staw (1976) documented "escalation of commitment": decision-makers become psychologically locked into failing courses of action through sunk costs and self-justification. Repenning and Sterman (2002) modeled this as a "capability trap"—a reinforcing feedback loop where short-term pressures crowd out improvement investments, degrading long-term performance.

**Current Follow-on.** Entrepreneurship scholars have operationalized these insights. Camuffo et al. (2020) demonstrated through randomized trials that scientific approaches—systematic hypothesis testing—improve venture outcomes. Yet Kirtley and O'Mahony (2023) show that pivoting remains difficult: it unfolds as a gradual process, not sudden epiphany, with founders retaining old strategy elements longer than efficient.

### Strand C: Strategic Ambiguity and Stakeholder Coordination

**Classical Origin.** Eisenberg (1984) introduced "strategic ambiguity" as an organizational communication strategy. Vague messages enable "unified diversity": stakeholders with different interests can each project their own interpretation onto a common vision, enabling coordination without explicit agreement.

**Development.** Subsequent work traced ambiguity's temporal dynamics. Abdallah (2014) identified the "double edge": ambiguity that enables early coalition-building can trap organizations later as stakeholders resist reinterpretations that threaten their particular understanding. Aumann (1976) provided formal foundations: rational agents with different priors can "agree to disagree" indefinitely.

**Current Follow-on.** Van den Steen (2004) resolved an apparent puzzle: founders' optimism is not cognitive bias but rational selection—those who choose to pursue a venture are, by definition, more optimistic about it. This creates systematic homophily: investors who fund share the founder's beliefs. Kamenica (2011) formalized "Bayesian persuasion": strategic information disclosure shapes receivers' beliefs. Together, these explain how funding creates echo chambers of like-minded believers.

### Strand D: Identity and Strategic Change

**Classical Origin.** Cyert and March (1963) established that organizations are coalitions with divergent goals, making adaptation politically contested. Nelson and Winter (1982) showed how routines create path dependence, constraining future options.

**Development.** Dynamic capabilities research sought escape routes. Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) argued that competitive advantage requires capabilities to reconfigure resources. Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) specified these as identifiable processes—strategic decision-making, alliance formation—that can be developed and deployed.

**Current Follow-on.** Zuzul and Tripsas (2020) explain variation in adaptation through founder identity. "Discoverers"—founders who define themselves through learning—reposition readily when evidence suggests change. "Revolutionaries"—founders whose identity is bound to a specific vision—resist repositioning even when warranted. The golden cage mechanism operates most powerfully on Revolutionaries: their identity is fused with the funded strategy.

### Strand E: Growth and Scaling

**Classical Origin.** Fine (1986) demonstrated the dynamic relationship between quality investment and learning: higher initial quality accelerates learning curves, enabling long-run cost reduction. This challenged the static view that quality and cost trade off.

**Development.** Van Mieghem (1998) extended flexibility analysis to capacity investment: flexible capacity has value even when demands are correlated, because margin differentials—not just demand pooling—drive value. The operations strategy literature (Van Mieghem, 2008) synthesized these insights: scaling requires matching capability growth to market growth.

**Current Follow-on.** Fine (2024) applies these principles to entrepreneurship: "Scale it = Grow in parallel your market size and your production and delivery capability." Successful scaling is not just demand generation but coordinated expansion of operational capacity. Ventures that scale market faster than capability face quality crises; those that scale capability faster than market waste resources.
```
